03 — Failure Mode Analysis (Evidence-Based)
Why Every AI Tool Tried Has Failed GMP Managers
These are not anecdotal complaints — each failure mode has a documented regulatory or scientific basis. This is why a Sanofi colleague with access to modern tools still can't use AI effectively for GMP correctness checking.

1. Hallucination in a Zero-Tolerance Environment

LLMs generate plausible-sounding but factually wrong content. In GMP, a fabricated regulatory reference or incorrect batch limit passes visual inspection and becomes a QMS record. FDA's April 2026 warning letter explicitly cited this as an enforcement violation.

PMC: Regulatory Perspectives for AI/ML in GMP Environments (PMC12195787); FDA Warning Letter, Apr 2026

2. Probabilistic Output vs. GMP Determinism

GMP requires determinism: the same process must produce the same documented outcome every time. LLMs are probabilistic — the same prompt yields different outputs. EMA Annex 22 draft (Jul 2025) explicitly restricts generative AI from critical GMP processes for this reason.

EMA Annex 22 Draft, Jul 2025; PDA Journal of Pharma Science & Technology, Feb 2026

3. Automation Bias Amplifies Risk

Research shows reviewers apply less scrutiny to AI output that looks authoritative — "automation bias." In GMP, a complete-looking AI-generated procedure can silently omit required steps (e.g., process validation) with no signal. This makes bad AI worse than no AI.

ISPE Pharmaceutical Engineering: "What You're Not Told by AI" (ispeak, 2026)

4. No Access to Company's Own Documents

GMP correctness is always relative to the company's own controlled documents — their approved master batch records, site-specific SOPs, approved specifications. Generic AI tools have no access to these. They check against public regulatory language, not the firm's actual approved procedures.

IntuitionLabs: AI in Pharmaceutical Quality Compliance, 2025; EY GxP AI report, 2025

5. No 21 CFR Part 11 Compliant Audit Trail

Any computer system used in GMP record-keeping must maintain a complete, tamper-evident, time-stamped audit trail attributable to a specific person (21 CFR Part 11). Generic AI chat tools produce no such trail — making their use legally inadmissible in FDA inspections.

21 CFR Part 11 (Electronic Records; Electronic Signatures); EMA Annex 11 (computerised systems)

6. Wrong Framing: Replacement vs. Augmentation

Tools tried to replace the reviewer — taking autonomous action. The correct framing is augmentation: pre-read the document, flag issues, draft with citations, require human confirmation before any finding enters the QMS. Harvey and Ketryx both operate on this model.

FDA: "Human oversight is required before AI output becomes a controlled document" — enforcement guidance, Apr 2026
⚠️
April 2026: FDA's First AI cGMP Warning Letter
The FDA issued its first warning letter explicitly citing inappropriate AI use in drug manufacturing. Cited violations included: AI outputs used without adequate human oversight, unvalidated AI-generated QC decisions, and absent audit trails for AI actions. 05 — System Design
The Architecture That Would Actually Work
Two non-negotiable constraints define the entire architecture: (1) regulatory validity — every AI action must be attributable, auditable, and human-approved before entering the QMS; (2) correctness — outputs must be grounded in the company's own controlled documents, never in generic web knowledge.

Layer 1: Deterministic Verification (Non-AI)

Run first, before any LLM touches the document. ALCOA+ completeness check (missing signatures, unsigned corrections, blank entries). Numerical limit comparison against master batch record specifications. SOP version verification against QMS current approved versions. Step sequence audit (all required steps present and in order). These are rule-based — deterministic, always correct, fast, and fully validatable under 21 CFR Part 11.

Layer 2: RAG Intelligence (AI — Non-Critical per Annex 22)

RAG over the company's indexed controlled documents (SOPs, master batch records, deviation history, CAPA library). Every answer is citation-grounded — the system returns the exact source document, section, and version. The reviewer verifies citations, not the AI's judgment. LLM is fine-tuned on GMP regulatory corpus (ICH Q10, 21 CFR 210/211, EMA GMP, Annex 22). Semantic deviation matching finds prior investigations for the same root cause pattern.

Layer 3: GMP Workflow Agents

Batch Record Pre-Read: AI reads full BMR, produces structured findings report (each issue categorized, cited, severity graded) — human confirms or dismisses each finding before it becomes a QMS record.

CAPA Draft Engine: Given deviation + root cause, generate a CAPA with regulatory-ready language, proposed actions, owners, effectiveness criteria — pre-populated from similar prior CAPAs in the company's history.

APQR Auto-Assembly: Query document index to auto-populate all APQR sections from the year's batch data, deviations, and stability records.

Layer 4: Compliance & Governance

21 CFR Part 11 Audit Trail: Every AI action logged — what was requested, generated, reviewed, and accepted/rejected, with timestamp and electronic signature.

Human-in-the-Loop by Design: No AI output enters the QMS without explicit human approval. AI produces "draft finding"; human converts it to "official record" via an approval action.

Validation Package Included: IQ/OQ/PQ documents pre-written — the single biggest barrier to enterprise adoption eliminated.
